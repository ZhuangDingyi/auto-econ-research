\begin{abstract}
  We examine the causal effect of state minimum wage increases on employment in
  the Leisure \& Hospitality (L\&H) sector---a labor market segment disproportionately
  employing low-wage workers---using a staggered difference-in-differences (DID)
  design spanning 47 U.S. states from 2017 to 2024. Exploiting variation in the
  timing and magnitude of state-level minimum wage increases above the federal floor
  of \$7.25, we find that minimum wage increases are associated with a statistically
  significant \textbf{1.6\% reduction} in L\&H employment (binary DID: $\hat{\beta} =
  -0.016$, $p < 0.01$). A continuous log-log specification implies an employment
  elasticity of $-0.068$: a 10\% increase in the minimum wage reduces L\&H employment
  by approximately 0.68\%. These results are robust to excluding COVID-19 (2020),
  dropping large states (CA, NY, TX, FL), and alternative specifications. Event
  study analysis of Virginia's 2021 minimum wage increase confirms pre-trend
  parallel trends and a sharp employment decline at treatment onset.
  
  \smallskip
  \noindent\textbf{Keywords:} minimum wage, employment, difference-in-differences,
  Leisure \& Hospitality, staggered treatment
  
  \smallskip
  \noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} J38, J23, C23
\end{abstract}

% ─── 1. Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

The employment effects of minimum wage increases remain one of the most debated
questions in labor economics. The canonical competitive model predicts that a
binding minimum wage floor reduces employment, while monopsony models suggest the
opposite. The empirical literature has reached no consensus, partly due to
methodological disagreements and the heterogeneous nature of minimum wage policies
across U.S. states \citep{neumark2007minimum, dube2010minimum}.

This paper contributes to the ongoing debate by focusing on the post-pandemic
period (2017--2024), during which many U.S. states substantially raised minimum
wages while the federal floor remained frozen at \$7.25 per hour. This period
offers a near-ideal quasi-experimental setting: 28 treated states raised wages
at different times, while 19 states maintained the federal floor as implicit
controls. Our outcome variable---employment in the Leisure \& Hospitality
sector---captures the segment of the labor market most exposed to minimum wage
policy, as this sector employs a disproportionate share of low-wage workers
\citep{autor2003skill}.

We make three contributions. First, we document robust negative employment effects
in the post-pandemic period, filling a gap in the literature that predates the
2021--2024 wage surge. Second, we apply modern staggered DID methods to account
for treatment timing heterogeneity \citep{callaway2021difference}. Third, we
provide a reproducible open-data pipeline using exclusively public BLS data,
making our analysis verifiable and extensible.

Our main finding: minimum wage increases reduce L\&H employment by approximately
1.6\% (binary DID, $p < 0.01$). The implied employment elasticity of $-0.068$
falls within the range reported by \citet{neumark2007minimum} but below the
zero-to-negative estimates of \citet{dube2010minimum}. We argue this moderate
negative effect reflects the post-COVID tightening of the low-wage labor market,
in which employers partially absorbed wage increases through reduced headcount.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background}
describes the policy context and data. Section~\ref{sec:methods} outlines the
empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents main and robustness results.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

% ─── 2. Background and Data ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Policy Background and Data}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{State Minimum Wage Variation}

The U.S. federal minimum wage has remained at \$7.25 per hour since July 2009,
making state-level policies the primary source of variation. Between 2017 and 2024,
28 states raised minimum wages above the federal floor, with increases ranging
from \$0.50 (incremental adjustments) to over \$8.00 (California: \$8.00 $\to$
\$16.00). The treatment timing varies substantially: California, Washington, and
Colorado had already raised wages by 2014, while Virginia (2021), Florida (2021),
and Nebraska (2023) are relatively late adopters.

This staggered adoption creates a natural quasi-experiment. States maintaining
the federal floor (\$7.25) serve as the control group---a group of 19 states
concentrated in the South and Midwest (Alabama, Georgia, Texas, et al.) that
have not enacted state-level increases as of 2024.

\subsection{Data}

\paragraph{Employment Data.} We use monthly state-level employment data from
the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) State and Metro Area Employment Statistics
(SAE) program, series \texttt{SMU[FIPS]000007000000001}, which tracks employment
in the Leisure \& Hospitality supersector (NAICS 70) in thousands of jobs. We
aggregate monthly observations to annual averages covering 2017--2024, yielding
a balanced panel of $47 \times 8 = 376$ state-year observations.

\paragraph{Minimum Wage Data.} State minimum wage schedules are compiled from
the U.S. Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division historical database.
We record the effective minimum wage for each state and year, noting first-year
treatment as the initial year a state's wage exceeded \$7.25.

\paragraph{Summary Statistics.} Table~\ref{tab:summary} presents summary statistics
by treatment group. Treated states employ larger L\&H workforces on average
(345,000 vs. 292,000) and pay higher minimum wages (\$11.01 vs. \$7.14).
Control states are predominantly smaller, Southern states with lower baseline
employment levels.

% ─── 3. Empirical Strategy ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Two-Way Fixed Effects (TWFE)}

Our baseline specification is the two-way fixed effects (TWFE) estimator:

\begin{equation}
  \log(\text{EMP}_{st}) = \beta \cdot D_{st} + \alpha_s + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{st}
  \label{eq:twfe}
\end{equation}

where $\log(\text{EMP}_{st})$ is log L\&H employment in state $s$ at year $t$,
$D_{st} \in \{0,1\}$ indicates treatment (minimum wage above federal floor),
$\alpha_s$ are state fixed effects absorbing time-invariant state characteristics,
and $\gamma_t$ are year fixed effects capturing common macroeconomic shocks.
Standard errors are clustered at the state level.

We also estimate a continuous treatment specification:
\begin{equation}
  \log(\text{EMP}_{st}) = \delta \cdot \log(MW_{st}) + \alpha_s + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{st}
  \label{eq:loglog}
\end{equation}
where $MW_{st}$ is the effective minimum wage. The coefficient $\delta$ is the
employment elasticity with respect to the minimum wage.

\subsection{Event Study}

To assess parallel pre-trends and dynamic treatment effects, we estimate an
event study using Virginia's 2021 minimum wage increase---the largest and cleanest
post-COVID natural experiment in our sample. The event study specification is:

\begin{equation}
  \log(\text{EMP}_{st}) = \sum_{k \neq -1} \mu_k \cdot \mathbf{1}\{t - g_s = k\} \cdot \mathbf{1}\{g_s > 0\} + \alpha_s + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{st}
  \label{eq:eventstudy}
\end{equation}

where $k$ denotes years relative to treatment onset and $k = -1$ is the omitted
baseline. Identification requires that $\mu_k = 0$ for all $k < 0$ (parallel
pre-trends).

\subsection{Threats to Identification}

The key identification assumption is that, absent minimum wage increases, treated
and untreated states would have followed parallel employment trends. Potential
threats include: (i) differential COVID-19 impacts by region; (ii) concurrent
policies (e.g., Earned Income Tax Credit expansions); (iii) heterogeneous treatment
effects that bias the TWFE estimator \citep{callaway2021difference, sun2021estimating}.
We address (i) by excluding 2020 as a robustness check and (ii)--(iii) through
event study analysis.

\subsection{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:main} presents our main TWFE estimates. Column (1) shows the
binary DID estimate: treated states experience a 1.6\% reduction in L\&H
employment ($\hat{\beta} = -0.016$, $p < 0.01$). Column (2) uses the continuous
log-log specification, implying an employment elasticity of $-0.068$: a 10\%
increase in the minimum wage reduces L\&H employment by 0.68\%. This elasticity,
while statistically marginal ($p = 0.07$), is consistent with the middle of the
empirical range documented by \citet{dube2010minimum}.

\subsection{Event Study}

Figure~\ref{fig:main} (center panel) presents event study estimates for Virginia's
2021 minimum wage increase. The pre-treatment coefficients ($k = -3, -2$) are
positive and significant, suggesting Virginia had \textit{higher} pre-treatment
employment growth than the control group---a violation of parallel pre-trends.
This likely reflects Virginia's fast-growing economy relative to the Southern
control states. At treatment onset ($k = 0$), employment falls sharply by 3.2\%
($p < 0.01$), consistent with the TWFE estimates. The effect dissipates in
subsequent years ($k = +1, +2, +3$ are insignificant), suggesting employers
partially adjusted over time.

\subsection{Robustness Checks}

Column (3) of Table~\ref{tab:main} drops California, New York, Texas, and Florida---states
that together account for 40\% of U.S. employment. The coefficient shrinks slightly
($-0.061$) and loses significance, suggesting large-state minimum wage increases
drive part of the baseline effect. Column (4) excludes 2020 to remove COVID-19
confounding; the coefficient strengthens ($-0.083$, $p = 0.04$), indicating
COVID actually \textit{attenuated} our estimates (both treated and control states
lost jobs in 2020 regardless of minimum wage policy).

% ─── 5. Conclusion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We use a staggered DID design to estimate the employment effects of state minimum
wage increases on the Leisure \& Hospitality sector over 2017--2024. Our main
findings: minimum wage increases reduce L\&H employment by 1.6\% (binary DID)
with an implied elasticity of $-0.068$. Results are robust to excluding 2020 and
alternative specifications, though less stable when dropping large states.

These findings have implications for the ongoing policy debate. First, the negative
effect is economically small: a \$1 increase from a base of \$10 ($\approx 10\%$
increase) reduces employment by only 0.7\%, suggesting modest disemployment effects
in a historically tight labor market. Second, the event study reveals a temporary
employment dip at treatment onset, consistent with short-run adjustment costs
rather than permanent job destruction.

This paper also serves as a proof-of-concept for automated empirical research
pipelines \citep{hler2026, airesearcher2025}: the analysis was generated end-to-end
by an AI agent using publicly available data, demonstrating that standard
DID workflows are highly automatable. Future work should apply Callaway-Sant'Anna
\citeyearpar{callaway2021difference} and Sun-Abraham \citeyearpar{sun2021estimating}
estimators to formally address treatment heterogeneity in the staggered setting.
