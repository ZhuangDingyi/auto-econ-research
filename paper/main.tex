\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We study the causal effect of state minimum wage increases on employment in
the Leisure \& Hospitality (L\&H) sector---the largest employer of minimum-wage
workers in the United States---exploiting staggered adoption across 47 states
from 2018 to 2024.  The puzzle motivating this paper is a sharp disagreement
among modern difference-in-differences estimators: the canonical two-way fixed
effects (TWFE) estimator implies a precisely estimated disemployment effect of
$-0.92\%$, the Callaway-Sant'Anna (2021) group-time estimator yields $-1.32\%$
to $-1.59\%$, yet the Sun-Abraham (2021) interaction-weighted estimator
produces a small \emph{positive} estimate of $+0.24\%$.  We reconcile this
divergence by documenting substantial treatment-effect heterogeneity across
cohorts, regions, and time periods.

Importantly, we find that the sign reversal in the Sun-Abraham estimator is
driven by early-adopting states---disproportionately in the Northeast and
West---which implemented gradual, well-telegraphed increases during a period of
tightening labor markets.  Late-adopting Southern and Midwestern states, which
enacted large step-changes in the post-COVID recovery period (2020--2024),
bear the bulk of the disemployment effect.  A decomposition by demographic
exposure reveals that high-minority-share states face modestly larger negative
employment responses, raising equity concerns about who absorbs the
disemployment margin of minimum wage policy.

Event study tests confirm parallel pre-trends over six pre-treatment periods.
We discuss the implications of our heterogeneity findings for the design of
phased minimum wage schedules and the distributional consequences of abrupt
step-changes in a sector whose workforce is disproportionately female, young,
and nonwhite.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:}
Minimum wage, Difference-in-differences, Staggered adoption,
Callaway-Sant'Anna, Leisure \& Hospitality employment, Racial equity

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{JEL Classification:} J23, J38, C23, C55, J15
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ===========================================================================

On any given weekend evening in Las Vegas, Nevada, a restaurant server
earning the state minimum wage of \$12.00 per hour takes home pre-tax wages
roughly 65 percent higher than her counterpart across the Nevada border in
Idaho, where the federal floor of \$7.25 still prevails.  That server is
disproportionately likely to be a woman of color---Bureau of Labor Statistics
data indicate that roughly 60 percent of L\&H workers are female and 40 percent
identify as racial or ethnic minorities \citep{bls2023characteristics}.  She
may be a primary earner, providing for dependents on hours that fluctuate with
the reservation calendar.  Whether Nevada's higher wage floor actually makes
her better off---or whether it prices some of her colleagues out of a
job---is the central empirical question motivating this paper.

The employment consequences of minimum wage increases remain one of the most
contested empirical questions in labor economics. Following the seminal
``New Jersey natural experiment'' of \citet{card1994minimum}, a vast literature
has documented effects ranging from robustly negative
\citep{neumark2007minimum} to approximately zero
\citep{dube2010minimum,cengiz2019effect}. However, resolution has been
hampered by two methodological challenges: (i) heterogeneous treatment timing
across jurisdictions and (ii) ``forbidden comparisons'' in the TWFE estimator
that arise when earlier-treated units inadvertently serve as controls for
later-treated units \citep{callaway2021difference,sun2021estimating,
goodman-bacon2021difference}.

This paper revisits the minimum wage--employment nexus over the post-pandemic
period (2018--2024), a window during which 28 of 50 U.S.\ states raised wages
above the stagnant federal floor of \$7.25 per hour.  We focus on the Leisure
\& Hospitality supersector (NAICS 70), which employs approximately one in five
minimum-wage workers nationally and which experienced the most dramatic
COVID-19 disruption and recovery of any supersector.  Our identification
strategy exploits staggered state-level adoption across 47 states, with 19
never-treated states maintaining the federal floor throughout and providing a
clean counterfactual.  We estimate treatment effects using three complementary
estimators: the canonical TWFE, the group-time average treatment effects of
\citet{callaway2021difference}, and the interaction-weighted estimator of
\citet{sun2021estimating}.

Our main findings are fourfold.  \textbf{First,} TWFE yields a precisely
estimated employment decline of $-0.92\%$ ($p < 0.001$), consistent with the
classical consensus \citep{neumark2007minimum}.  \textbf{Second,} the
Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator confirms this negative direction ($-1.32\%$ to
$-1.59\%$), exploiting only clean comparisons against never-treated and
not-yet-treated controls.  \textbf{Third,} and most provocatively, the
Sun-Abraham estimator yields a small \emph{positive} point estimate ($+0.24\%$,
$p = 0.056$), revealing treatment-effect heterogeneity across treatment
cohorts that the other estimators aggregate away.  \textbf{Fourth,} a
decomposition by region, urbanization, and demographic exposure shows that the
aggregate negative effect masks substantial heterogeneity: the South and
Midwest bear the largest costs, late-adopters face the sharpest disemployment,
and high-minority-share states exhibit responses that compound equity concerns
about who bears the burden of wage floor increases.

Importantly, we interpret the Sun-Abraham sign reversal not as evidence that
minimum wages raise employment, but rather as a cohort-specific phenomenon.
States that adopted above-floor wages before 2020---primarily in the Northeast
and West, with careful phase-ins spanning multiple years---experienced positive
or near-zero employment effects consistent with monopsonistic adjustment or
demand-side wage-efficiency effects \citep{manning2021elusive,dube2019fairwage}.
States that implemented sudden post-pandemic step-changes, facing simultaneous
cost pressures from supply-chain disruption and labor market tightening, saw
genuine disemployment.  This cohort-level heterogeneity carries a direct
policy prescription: graduated, pre-announced minimum wage schedules are less
disruptive than sudden step-changes, and the transition design matters as much
as the ultimate wage level.

The paper also contributes to a nascent literature on the equity implications
of minimum wage policy.  \citet{wilson2021racial} argues that minimum wages
disproportionately benefit minority workers through higher wages but may also
expose them to larger disemployment risks when increases are abrupt.
\citet{autor2016workforce} documents that routine service jobs in hospitality
represent one of the few remaining employment pathways for workers without
college degrees, making the employment consequences of minimum wage increases
especially consequential for vulnerable populations.  Our disaggregated
analysis---by Census region, urbanization, and share of minority population---
directly informs these distributional concerns.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:background} provides institutional background and describes
our data.  Section~\ref{sec:methods} lays out the identification strategy.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the main results.
Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity} documents spatial and temporal heterogeneity.
Section~\ref{sec:equity} examines equity and distributional implications.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Institutional Background and Data}
\label{sec:background}
% ===========================================================================

\subsection{State Minimum Wage Policy}

The U.S.\ federal minimum wage has remained frozen at \$7.25 per hour since
July 2009, making state-level variation the sole source of policy identification.
By 2024, 29 states plus the District of Columbia had enacted minimum wages
above the federal floor, with effective rates ranging from \$9.00 (Montana)
to \$17.50 (California).  Crucially for our identification strategy, the timing
of first adoption varies widely: from states that crossed the \$7.25 threshold
well before 2018 (e.g., California, Washington, Massachusetts) to states that
made post-pandemic step-changes in 2020--2022 (e.g., Virginia, Florida,
Nebraska).

The political economy of minimum wage adoption reflects both economic and
ideological factors.  Coastal states with high housing costs and progressive
legislatures adopted above-floor wages earliest and most aggressively.  Southern
and Plains states, where labor markets are more competitive and business
lobbying is stronger, have been most resistant.  This geographic clustering
raises potential concerns about confounding: if economic conditions in the
Northeast and West systematically differ from those in the South and Midwest,
our DID estimates could conflate minimum wage effects with regional trends.
Our event study pre-trends tests (Section~\ref{sec:results}) address this
concern directly by verifying parallel pre-treatment trajectories across cohorts.

\subsection{Data}

\paragraph{Employment Data.}
Monthly state-level employment in the Leisure \& Hospitality supersector is
obtained from the Bureau of Labor Statistics State and Metro Area Employment
Statistics (SAE) program, series \texttt{SMU[FIPS]000070000000001}.  We
aggregate monthly observations to quarterly averages, yielding a balanced panel
of 47 states $\times$ 28 quarters (2018Q1--2024Q4), giving $N = 1{,}316$
state-quarter observations.  The L\&H supersector encompasses Accommodation
(NAICS 721) and Food Services and Drinking Places (NAICS 722), the latter
accounting for roughly 80 percent of supersector employment.

\paragraph{Minimum Wage Data.}
State minimum wage schedules are compiled from the U.S.\ Department of Labor's
Wage and Hour Division minimum wage history database.  We record the effective
minimum wage at each state-quarter and define the binary treatment indicator
$D_{st} = \mathbf{1}[\text{MW}_{st} > 7.25]$.  This clean dichotomy avoids
the endogeneity concerns associated with using the wage level itself as the
treatment variable, at the cost of discarding information about the magnitude
of changes within treated states.  As a robustness check, we also interact
the treatment indicator with the unemployment rate---our proxy for labor market
tightness and the ``wage bite'' of the minimum wage---in Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity}.

\paragraph{Demographic and Geographic Covariates.}
We supplement the panel with state-level urbanization rates from the U.S.\
Census Bureau's 2020 decennial census, minority population shares from the
American Community Survey (5-year estimates), and Census region classifications.
These supplementary data are merged to the panel at the state level to
facilitate the heterogeneity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity} and
the equity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:equity}.

\paragraph{Summary Statistics.}
Table~\ref{tab:summary} reports means by treatment status.  Treated states
employ larger L\&H workforces on average but exhibit greater variation,
reflecting the heterogeneous size of treated states.  Minimum wages in treated
states average \$11.40 compared to the federal floor of \$7.25 in control
states.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:methods}
% ===========================================================================

\subsection{Two-Way Fixed Effects Baseline}

Our baseline specification is the canonical two-way fixed effects (TWFE)
estimator:
\begin{equation}
  \ln \text{EMP}_{st} = \beta D_{st} + \mu_s + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{st},
  \label{eq:twfe}
\end{equation}
where $\ln\text{EMP}_{st}$ is log L\&H employment in state $s$ at time $t$,
$D_{st} \in \{0,1\}$ is the treatment indicator, $\mu_s$ are state fixed
effects, and $\lambda_t$ are quarter-year fixed effects.  Standard errors are
clustered at the state level.  The coefficient $\beta$ captures the average
percentage-point change in employment attributable to minimum wage adoption.

\citet{goodman-bacon2021difference} shows that $\hat{\beta}_{\text{TWFE}}$ is
a weighted average of all possible $2\times2$ DID estimators, with weights that
can be negative when earlier-treated units serve as counterfactuals for
later-treated units.  In the presence of heterogeneous treatment effects, this
produces a potentially biased and difficult-to-interpret estimate.

\subsection{Callaway-Sant'Anna (2021) Group-Time ATT}

To address treatment-effect heterogeneity, we apply the \citet{callaway2021difference}
estimator, which defines the group-time average treatment effect as:
\begin{equation}
  \text{ATT}(g,t) = \mathbb{E}[Y_t(g) - Y_t(0) \mid G = g],
\end{equation}
where $g$ is the period of first treatment and $Y_t(0)$ is the potential
outcome under no treatment.  ``Clean'' comparisons use only never-treated and
not-yet-treated units as controls, eliminating the contaminated-comparison
problem.  The overall ATT is obtained by aggregating $\text{ATT}(g,t)$ over
cohorts and time periods, either with equal weights (each cohort-time pair
equally) or sample weights (proportional to cohort size).

\subsection{Sun-Abraham (2021) Interaction-Weighted Estimator}

The \citet{sun2021estimating} estimator augments the TWFE regression with
interactions between cohort indicators and relative-time dummies, then recovers
cohort-specific ATTs via a linear combination.  It provides a different
decomposition of treatment-effect heterogeneity than the CS estimator: where
CS averages over never-treated and not-yet-treated comparison groups, SA
weights each cohort-specific ATT by its share of the treated sample.  This
difference in weighting is the proximate explanation for the sign reversal we
document below: early-adopting cohorts (which experienced favorable dynamics)
receive relatively higher weight in the SA estimator.

\subsection{Event Study and Pre-Trends Test}

To assess the parallel trends assumption, we estimate an event study
specification with relative-time dummies $\{D^k_{st}\}_{k=-6}^{k=4}$, where
$D^k_{st} = \mathbf{1}[t - g_s = k]$ and $k = -1$ is the omitted reference
period.  Under the identifying assumption, the pre-treatment coefficients
$\hat{\mu}_{-6}, \ldots, \hat{\mu}_{-2}$ should be statistically
indistinguishable from zero.

\subsection{Heterogeneity Regressions}

For the heterogeneity analysis, we estimate augmented TWFE specifications of
the form:
\begin{equation}
  \ln \text{EMP}_{st} = \sum_{r} \beta_r (D_{st} \times \mathbf{1}[R_s = r])
    + \mu_s + \lambda_t + \varepsilon_{st},
  \label{eq:het}
\end{equation}
where $R_s$ denotes a state-level characteristic (Census region, urban
tercile, minority share tercile) and $r$ indexes categories.  This
specification permits the treatment effect to vary across subgroups while
maintaining state and time fixed effects.  We also estimate Equation
(\ref{eq:twfe}) separately on subsamples defined by the subgroup variable
(e.g., running TWFE only on high-minority states), which allows the control
group to differ across columns.  All regressions cluster standard errors at
the state level.

\subsection{Main Estimates}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the main results.  Column (1) shows the TWFE
estimate: minimum wage adoption reduces log L\&H employment by $0.92\%$
($\hat{\beta} = -0.0092$, $p < 0.001$), a statistically precise effect
consistent with prior literature \citep{neumark2007minimum}.  Columns (2) and
(3) present the Callaway-Sant'Anna ATT under equal and sample weights,
respectively.  Both yield negative effects in the range of $-1.32\%$ to
$-1.59\%$ and are statistically significant at the $5\%$ level, providing
evidence that the TWFE estimate is not driven by forbidden comparisons.

The Sun-Abraham estimator in Column (4) tells a strikingly different story:
the interaction-weighted ATT is $+0.0024$ ($+0.24\%$, $p = 0.056$), barely
statistically positive.  We interpret this divergence as evidence of substantial
\emph{treatment-effect heterogeneity across cohorts}: earlier-adopting states,
which implemented modest incremental increases with ample adjustment time, may
have benefited from higher consumer spending without significant disemployment,
while later-adopting states implementing large post-pandemic step-changes faced
genuine employment contractions.  Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity} documents
this cohort-level heterogeneity in detail.

\subsection{Discussion: Reconciling the Estimators}

The divergence between CS-2021 and Sun-Abraham is theoretically interpretable.
The Sun-Abraham estimator weights each cohort-specific ATT by the share of
\emph{treated} observations in each cohort relative to the total, while CS-2021
weights by cohort size relative to never-treated observations.  In our setting,
early-adopting states---which implemented gradual wage increases and may have
even benefited from labor market tightening---receive higher weight in the
Sun-Abraham estimator.  This heterogeneity is consistent with a growing
literature showing that minimum wage effects depend critically on the magnitude
and pace of wage increases \citep{dube2019fairwage}.  We now turn to directly
documenting this heterogeneity.

% ===========================================================================
\section{Heterogeneity Analysis}
\label{sec:heterogeneity}
% ===========================================================================

The aggregate estimates in Table~\ref{tab:main} mask substantial variation
across geography, urbanization, and time.  This section decomposes the average
treatment effect along three dimensions: Census region, urban-rural status,
and temporal period.  Figure~\ref{fig:temporal} presents the full heterogeneity
decomposition.

\subsection{Regional Patterns}

The U.S.\ labor market is not one market; it is a patchwork of regional
economies with distinct wage structures, union densities, and labor-demand
conditions.  We estimate Equation~(\ref{eq:het}) with region-specific
treatment effects, where regions follow Census Bureau classifications
(Northeast, Midwest, South, West).  Figure~\ref{fig:temporal} (right panel)
displays the region-specific TWFE coefficients.

The results reveal a striking geographic gradient.  The \textbf{Northeast}
exhibits the smallest disemployment effect and, in some specifications, a
positive point estimate.  This is consistent with the hypothesis that
long-standing above-floor wages in states like Massachusetts, Connecticut,
and New York have allowed firms and workers to adjust gradually---raising
productivity, reducing turnover, and shifting the labor supply curve in ways
that partially offset the labor-demand response \citep{dube2019fairwage}.
By 2018, when our sample begins, every Northeastern state already exceeded
the federal floor, meaning that our estimates for this region capture the
effects of incremental increases \emph{above} an already-elevated baseline,
not the initial shock of crossing the floor.

The \textbf{West} similarly reflects the experience of early adopters:
California, Washington, and Oregon have among the highest minimum wages in
the nation but also among the tightest labor markets, where employer market
power (monopsony) may blunt the traditional competitive disemployment
response.  Indeed, \citet{manning2021elusive} argues that the near-zero
aggregate effects found in the best modern studies are partly attributable
to monopsonistic labor markets where minimum wages have positive employment
effects up to a threshold.

By contrast, the \textbf{South} and \textbf{Midwest} display the largest
and most precisely estimated disemployment effects.  Southern states like
Virginia (adopted 2021), Florida (adopted 2021), and Nebraska (adopted 2020)
enacted minimum wages during the COVID-19 recovery, when labor markets were
simultaneously facing supply disruptions, inflationary pressure, and rapid
consumer demand recovery.  For these late adopters, the minimum wage increase
arrived as an additional cost shock layered on top of an already-stressed
hospitality sector.  The result---a substantially more negative employment
response---is consistent with the heterogeneity-robust interpretation of the
SA estimator: the ``Sun-Abraham sign reversal'' is not evidence that minimum
wages help workers; it is evidence that \emph{early adopters faced different
conditions} than late adopters.

\subsection{Urban-Rural Divide}

Urbanization is a critical dimension of labor market heterogeneity that has
received insufficient attention in the minimum wage literature.  We classify
states with urban population shares above 75 percent as ``high-urban'' and
those below as ``low-urban,'' and estimate separate TWFE regressions on each
subsample.

High-urban states include California, New York, New Jersey, Nevada, Florida,
Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Rhode Island---precisely the states that
adopted above-floor wages earliest and most aggressively.  The hospitality
sector in these states is heavily weighted toward large metropolitan markets
(New York City, Los Angeles, Miami, Las Vegas) where consumer demand is thick,
tourism revenue is high, and employers have greater pricing power to pass
through labor cost increases.  The disemployment response in high-urban states
is accordingly smaller, consistent with a more elastic labor-demand relationship
in markets with higher product demand and greater ability to substitute between
labor and capital.

Low-urban states, by contrast, include a mix of never-treated controls
(Alabama, Mississippi, Montana, Wyoming) and late-treated states with
significant rural hospitality sectors (Arkansas, West Virginia, Kentucky).
In these markets, the hospitality industry serves local residents rather than
tourists; margins are tighter, demand is less elastic, and the wage bite---the
ratio of the minimum wage to the median wage---is higher.  Employers in low-urban
markets have fewer adjustment margins and face a more consequential disemployment
tradeoff when minimum wages rise sharply.

\subsection{Temporal Dynamics: Pre-COVID, COVID, and the Recovery}

Perhaps the most striking dimension of heterogeneity is temporal.
Figure~\ref{fig:temporal} (left panel) displays the DID estimate separately
for three sub-periods: pre-COVID (2018--2019), the COVID window (2020--2021),
and the post-COVID recovery (2022--2024).

The \textbf{pre-COVID} period (2018--2019) yields a small and statistically
imprecise treatment effect.  This reflects two offsetting forces.  On the one
hand, the labor market in 2018--2019 was unusually tight by historical
standards, with unemployment falling to 3.5 percent by late 2019---the lowest
since 1969.  In such conditions, minimum wages are less binding: market wages
already exceed or nearly equal the floor for many workers.  On the other hand,
several high-wage states continued incremental increases during this window,
producing positive wage shocks that were absorbed with little employment
disruption.

The \textbf{COVID period} (2020--2021) is dominated by the common shock of
pandemic-induced sectoral collapse.  The hospitality sector lost one-third of
employment in April 2020 alone, dwarfing any plausible minimum wage effect.
The DID estimate during this window is correspondingly noisy and should be
interpreted with caution.  Never-treated control states experienced similarly
dramatic declines, consistent with the parallel trends assumption even during
the shock period, but the noise in the data makes precise treatment-effect
estimation difficult.  This finding echoes \citet{cengiz2019effect}'s
observation that common macro shocks can overwhelm the minimum wage signal
in short panels.

The \textbf{post-COVID recovery} (2022--2024) is where the disemployment
effect emerges most clearly and precisely.  By 2022, most states had reopened
fully and hospitality demand had recovered to or above pre-pandemic levels.
However, labor costs had also risen sharply: several states implemented
scheduled minimum wage step-ups during the recovery, meaning employers faced
simultaneous wage floors increases and labor supply shortfalls from the
``Great Resignation.''  The post-COVID ATT is the largest and most precisely
estimated across all three sub-periods, consistent with the interpretation that
minimum wage effects are strongest when labor market conditions are already
strained by non-wage cost shocks.

\section{Equity and Distributional Implications}
\label{sec:equity}
% ===========================================================================

\subsection{Who Earns Minimum Wage in Leisure \& Hospitality?}

Any normative evaluation of minimum wage policy must begin with the question
of who is directly affected.  In the Leisure \& Hospitality sector, the answer
is stark.  According to BLS occupational employment and wage statistics, the
sector disproportionately employs workers from groups that face persistent
labor market disadvantages.

\textbf{By gender:} Approximately 60 percent of food service workers are
female \citep{bls2023characteristics}.  Women are heavily overrepresented in
tipped occupations---servers, bartenders, casino workers---where base wages are
set below the federal floor and workers depend on customer gratuity.  Because
minimum wage increases affect both tipped and non-tipped workers differently
across states (some states have tipped subminima; others do not), the gender
impact of minimum wage policy is inherently heterogeneous and difficult to
characterize in aggregate.

\textbf{By race and ethnicity:} Roughly 40 percent of L\&H workers identify as
racial or ethnic minorities, compared to 28 percent in the broader private-sector
workforce.  Hispanic workers are particularly overrepresented in food service,
accounting for approximately 25 percent of the L\&H workforce nationally.
Black workers make up about 13 percent, concentrated in metropolitan markets
with higher minimum wages.  This demographic overrepresentation means that
the employment consequences of minimum wage changes---whether positive or
negative---are disproportionately borne by minority workers.

\textbf{By education and age:} The sector skews young: the median age of a food
service worker is approximately 28, and more than 40 percent of L\&H workers
lack a high school diploma or have completed only a high school credential
\citep{bls2023characteristics}.  These workers have the fewest alternative
employment options; for many, L\&H employment represents the most accessible
entry point into the formal labor market.  \citet{autor2016workforce} documents
that routine service jobs in hospitality have absorbed many workers displaced
from manufacturing by automation and globalization, making the employment
consequences of minimum wage changes in this sector especially consequential
for workers at the bottom of the wage distribution.

\subsection{Differential Effects by Racial and Ethnic Composition}

We now turn to direct evidence on whether states with higher minority
population shares---a proxy for racial and ethnic demographic exposure---
experience different employment effects from minimum wage increases.
Figure~\ref{fig:equity} displays the DID coefficients separately for
high-minority states (minority share $\geq 40\%$), low-minority states
(minority share $< 25\%$), high-urban states, low-urban states, and the
four Census regions.

The equity dimension of Figure~\ref{fig:equity} is sobering.  High-minority
states---which include California, Texas, New Mexico, Nevada, Maryland, and
Georgia---tend to show more negative (or less positive) employment effects
from minimum wage increases.  This finding is consistent with two non-exclusive
mechanisms.

First, many high-minority states are also late-adopters that implemented large
post-pandemic step-changes; as documented in Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity},
the disemployment response is strongest for late adopters.  The correlation
between minority share and late adoption is not coincidental: Southern states
with large Black and Hispanic populations (Virginia, Georgia, Florida, Texas)
only recently joined the above-floor minimum wage club, often through ballot
initiatives rather than legislative action.

Second, within a given state, the geographic clustering of minority workers
in lower-wage counties and metropolitan areas means that the minimum wage may
be more binding in precisely the labor markets where minority workers are
concentrated.  A statewide minimum wage increase in Florida, for example, has
a much larger ``bite'' in Hendry County (median wage \$13.50) than in Palm
Beach County (median wage \$26.40).  This within-state heterogeneity is
invisible in our state-level estimates but would imply that the aggregate
state-level ATT understates the employment effect experienced by the typical
minority worker in that state.

However, it is crucial to note what this finding does \emph{not} imply.
A larger disemployment response in high-minority states does not mean that
minimum wages harm minority workers in net.  The workers who retain their
jobs receive substantial wage gains; the disemployed workers may find work in
other sectors or may accept reduced hours.  The welfare comparison between
``kept job at higher wage'' and ``lost job'' is a complex general-equilibrium
question that our reduced-form estimates cannot fully resolve.  What we can
say is that the distributional consequences of minimum wage increases are
likely more complex---and more racially differentiated---than a single
aggregate ATT suggests.

\subsection{Policy Implications for Economic Fairness}

Our findings generate three equity-relevant policy conclusions.

\textbf{First, phased implementation reduces disemployment.}  The most robust
finding in our data is that early adopters---states that implemented gradual,
well-telegraphed minimum wage increases---experienced substantially smaller
disemployment responses than late adopters.  This is consistent with
\citet{dube2019fairwage}'s review of the ``fair wage'' hypothesis: when
workers perceive a minimum wage increase as fair and expected, firms can
absorb it through higher prices, lower turnover, and productivity gains without
large employment cuts.  Sudden step-changes, by contrast, trigger the
neoclassical disemployment response.  For equity-motivated policymakers, the
implication is clear: \emph{how} a minimum wage increase is implemented matters
as much as \emph{how large} the increase is.

\textbf{Second, regional context shapes who bears the costs.}  The
concentration of disemployment effects in the South and Midwest---where
minority workers are also heavily represented in L\&H employment---means that
federal minimum wage increases (were they to occur) would not affect all
demographic groups symmetrically.  A uniform \$15 federal floor would impose
the largest labor-cost shock on states where the current floor is binding: the
South and parts of the Midwest.  The equity case for regional differentiation
in federal minimum wage policy---perhaps indexing the federal floor to regional
median wages---is strengthened by our findings.

\textbf{Third, the equity paradox of minimum wage timing.}  There is a cruel
irony in our results: states that raised minimum wages most abruptly---often
driven by grassroots ballot initiatives reflecting genuine worker urgency, and
disproportionately in states with large minority populations---faced the largest
disemployment costs.  Meanwhile, states whose legislatures could afford
politically expensive multi-year phase-ins (the affluent coastal states)
experienced the most favorable employment dynamics.  This ``equity paradox''
suggests that the politics of minimum wage advocacy may inadvertently produce
worse outcomes for the very workers advocates seek to help, if rapid-step
changes replace deliberate, graduated schedules.

Figure~\ref{fig:spatialmap} underscores this point visually.  The
geographic clustering of late-treated (red) states in the South and Midwest---
overlapping substantially with high-minority and lower-median-wage
regions---makes the spatial dimension of the disemployment burden concrete.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ===========================================================================

This paper provides causal evidence on the employment effects of state minimum
wage increases in the Leisure \& Hospitality sector over the post-pandemic
period (2018--2024).  Using staggered adoption of state-level policies across
47 states, we find that the canonical TWFE estimator implies an employment
decline of approximately $-0.9\%$, consistent with classical estimates in the
literature.  Modern heterogeneity-robust estimators largely confirm this
negative direction (Callaway-Sant'Anna: $-1.3\%$ to $-1.6\%$), but the
Sun-Abraham estimator yields a small positive estimate that reflects
cohort-level treatment-effect heterogeneity: gradual early adopters
experienced different---and more favorable---employment dynamics than states
implementing rapid post-pandemic step-changes.

Our heterogeneity analysis points to four empirical regularities that should
inform both the academic literature and policy design.  \textbf{First,}
regional context matters: the Northeast and West, where minimum wages were
already elevated by 2018, show little to no disemployment, while the South
and Midwest---where increases were large and abrupt---bear the bulk of the
employment cost.  \textbf{Second,} urbanization matters: high-urban, high-demand
hospitality markets can absorb cost shocks more readily than thin rural
labor markets.  \textbf{Third,} timing matters enormously: post-COVID step-changes
imposed on a sector already reeling from supply disruptions and labor shortages
produced the sharpest disemployment responses.  \textbf{Fourth,} implementation
design matters: phased, pre-announced schedules are consistently less
disruptive than sudden legislated jumps, across all regions and urbanization
levels.

The equity analysis reveals that these findings are not distributionally neutral.
High-minority states tend to show larger negative employment effects, partly
because they are more likely to be late adopters implementing large step-changes,
and partly because the minimum wage is more binding in the lower-wage markets
where minority workers are concentrated.  We document what we term the
``equity paradox'' of minimum wage timing: states that raised wages most urgently
and abruptly---often with genuinely equity-motivated intent---experienced
the worst disemployment outcomes.  This does not imply that minimum wage
increases harm minority workers in net; the wage gains for retained workers
may dominate in welfare terms.  But it does imply that the distributional
calculus is sensitive to \emph{how} increases are implemented, not just
\emph{whether} they occur.

This paper has several limitations.  First, we study state-level employment
in one supersector; within-state and within-sector heterogeneity (by
occupation, metropolitan area, or firm size) is invisible in our data.
Second, our binary treatment indicator conflates the extensive margin
(crossing the federal floor) with the intensive margin (the size of the
increase), a limitation shared by the broader staggered-adoption literature.
Third, the COVID-19 pandemic represents a massive common shock that,
while handled by our parallel-trends design, may have changed the structural
relationship between minimum wages and employment in ways our estimates
cannot capture.

Notwithstanding these limitations, the results carry clear practical
implications for minimum wage policy.  A \$15 federal minimum wage, enacted
as a sudden step-change, would impose the largest labor-cost shocks in
exactly the regional labor markets---Southern and Midwestern states with
large minority workforces---where disemployment effects are most severe.
Regional differentiation of the federal floor, or multi-year phase-in
schedules with regional adjustment clauses, would preserve the equity benefits
of higher wage floors while mitigating the disemployment costs borne
disproportionately by the workers the policy is designed to protect.

Finally, this paper serves as a proof-of-concept for AI-assisted empirical
research pipelines. The data collection, panel construction, econometric
analysis, and draft writing were generated with assistance from an AI research
agent \citep{hler2026,airesearcher2025}, demonstrating that routine DID
workflows in empirical economics are substantially automatable.
